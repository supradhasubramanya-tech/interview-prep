# Behavioural Questions: Conflict, Issues, And Difficult Situations

Swedish HR and hiring managers often listen carefully for how you behave under pressure. They usually do not expect a dramatic story. They expect maturity: no blame, clear ownership, calm communication, respect for people, facts over emotion, and learning after the issue.

For Sandvik, connect every answer back to service ownership:

- Protect customer value and service reliability.
- Make facts and trade-offs visible.
- Keep stakeholders aligned.
- Escalate early when needed.
- Fix the immediate issue, then improve the system.

## Tone To Use

Use this style:

"I first tried to understand the facts and impact. Then I brought the right people together, clarified ownership, communicated transparently, and focused on recovery. Afterward, I looked at what we could improve in the process so the same issue would be less likely to repeat."

Avoid:
- Blaming a stakeholder, team, or supplier.
- Saying "I convinced everyone" too strongly.
- Making yourself sound like the only capable person.
- Overstating budget or contract ownership if it was not formally yours.

## 1. Tell Us About A Conflict With A Stakeholder

Use when they ask:
"Tell us about a time you had a conflict with a stakeholder."

Suggested answer:

"In one enterprise platform delivery, there was a conflict between a business stakeholder who wanted a customer commitment delivered quickly and the engineering team, who were concerned about capacity and release risk. The discussion was becoming more opinion-based than fact-based.

My role was to bring the conversation back to impact, options, and trade-offs. I clarified what the customer needed, what was truly mandatory for the release, what could be phased, and what risks engineering saw. I then created a simple view of options: deliver full scope later, deliver a smaller scope earlier, or accept a higher delivery risk with clear escalation. Once the trade-offs were visible, the stakeholders agreed to phase the scope and protect the critical customer outcome.

The result was a more realistic release plan and less friction between business and engineering. My learning was that conflict often reduces when people can see the same facts and decision options."

Sandvik connection:
"For My Sandvik, I would use the same approach when Sales, Marketing, IT, suppliers, and technical teams have different priorities on the portal roadmap."

## 2. Tell Us About A Difficult Customer Escalation

Use when they ask:
"Tell us about a time you handled a customer escalation or serious issue."

Suggested answer:

"At Qwikcilver/Pine Labs, I was involved in customer-facing platform delivery where customers depended on integrations, onboarding, and service performance. In one escalation situation, a customer issue was affecting confidence because the resolution path and ownership were not clear enough.

I first separated the immediate recovery from the longer-term fix. I aligned the internal teams on who owned investigation, communication, and next actions. I made sure the customer-facing update was clear and regular, without promising something before we had the facts. Internally, I tracked the issue through to closure and made sure the root cause and follow-up actions were captured.

The result was that the customer had clearer visibility, the internal teams had better ownership, and the issue was moved from escalation mode back into a controlled delivery and service process. I learned that in escalations, communication discipline is almost as important as technical resolution."

Sandvik connection:
"For a customer portal like My Sandvik, I would want customers and internal stakeholders to feel that there is clear ownership even when multiple teams and suppliers are involved."

## 3. Tell Us About A Time You Disagreed With A Technical Team

Use when they ask:
"Describe a situation where you disagreed with developers or technical experts."

Suggested answer:

"In platform delivery, I have had situations where the business wanted faster delivery, while the technical team wanted more time because of dependency or quality concerns. I try not to treat that as resistance. Usually, the technical team is protecting something important, such as reliability, maintainability, or security.

In one case, I asked the team to explain the risk in business language: what could happen, how likely it was, and what would be the impact if we ignored it. Then I worked with the Product Owner and stakeholders to decide what could be simplified or moved to a later release. This helped us keep the important customer outcome while reducing delivery risk.

The result was a better decision and more trust with the technical team. I showed that I was not only pushing for dates, but also protecting sustainable service quality."

Sandvik connection:
"As Service Owner, I would need exactly this balance: business value, roadmap speed, and stable service operation."

## 4. Tell Us About A Time You Had To Push Back Or Say No

Use when they ask:
"Tell us about a time you had to say no to a request."

Suggested answer:

"I had situations where stakeholders requested new scope close to a planned release. The request itself made sense from a business perspective, but adding it late would have increased risk and affected commitments already made to other stakeholders.

I did not just say no. I explained the impact on timeline, quality, and other commitments. Then I offered alternatives: include it in the next release, deliver a smaller version first, or reprioritize openly if it was more valuable than existing scope. This made the conversation about business trade-offs rather than personal disagreement.

The result was that the stakeholder accepted a phased approach. It protected the release and kept the relationship positive because the decision was transparent."

Sandvik connection:
"For My Sandvik, this is important because portal roadmaps can easily become overloaded. A Service Owner needs to protect priorities while still being business-oriented."

## 5. Tell Us About A Supplier Or Vendor Issue

Use when they ask:
"How do you handle a supplier who is not delivering?"

Suggested answer:

"In roles where vendors or external teams were part of delivery, I have handled situations where progress or quality was not meeting expectations. My approach is first to make the gap specific. Is it timeline, quality, communication, unclear requirements, dependency, or capacity?

Once the issue is clear, I set up a focused review with the supplier and internal stakeholders. I clarify the expected outcome, current gap, impact, owner, next action, and date. If needed, I increase the follow-up cadence and make risks visible in the governance forum. I try to be firm on the outcome but respectful in the relationship.

The result is usually faster recovery because the supplier understands exactly what needs to change and the business understands the risk and mitigation. If it does not improve, then escalation is based on evidence, not frustration."

Sandvik connection:
"This would be relevant for portal suppliers, application vendors, or support partners where SLA, delivery quality, and security expectations need active governance."

## 6. Tell Us About A Bad Situation That You Handled Well

Use when they ask:
"Tell us about a bad situation at work and how you handled it."

Suggested answer:

"A difficult situation I handled was when service requests and customer-facing delivery items were taking too long, and different stakeholders had different views of where the delays were happening. It was creating frustration because the issue was visible, but the flow was not.

I treated it as a service flow problem. I introduced Kanban-based visibility, WIP limits, clearer escalation paths, and regular review of blocked items. This helped the team see where work was waiting, which requests needed escalation, and where prioritization decisions were needed.

The result was a 50 percent reduction in service request turnaround time. More importantly, the conversation changed from blame to flow and ownership. That experience shaped how I think about service improvement: make the work visible, measure it, and improve it collaboratively."

Sandvik connection:
"For My Sandvik, I would use the same thinking for support tickets, incident trends, backlog flow, and customer-impacting improvements."

## 7. Tell Us About A Mistake Or Something That Did Not Go As Planned

Use when they ask:
"Tell us about a failure" or "What would you have done differently?"

Suggested answer:

"One thing I have learned from complex delivery is that risks can become escalations if they are discussed too late or only inside the delivery team. In one situation, dependencies and expectations were not aligned early enough, and it created pressure close to the planned delivery point.

My response was to bring the stakeholders together, clarify the actual status, separate must-have scope from nice-to-have scope, and reset the plan with clearer ownership. Afterward, I improved the reporting rhythm so risks, dependencies, and decisions were visible earlier.

The learning for me was that transparency has to start before a risk becomes an escalation. Today I prefer to show risks early, even when the message is uncomfortable, because it gives stakeholders time to make better decisions."

Sandvik connection:
"That is important in service ownership because late surprises can affect reliability, suppliers, business trust, and customer experience."

## 8. Tell Us About A Time You Managed Conflict Inside A Team

Use when they ask:
"How do you handle conflict between team members?"

Suggested answer:

"In cross-functional delivery, I have seen tension between roles when priorities were unclear, for example between product expectations, delivery capacity, and operational support needs. My first step is usually to speak with the people involved and understand whether the conflict is personal, role-based, or caused by unclear process.

Then I bring the discussion back to the shared goal and working agreement. I clarify decision rights, responsibilities, and what information is needed to move forward. If the conflict is about priorities, I use data such as business impact, SLA, dependency, risk, and capacity. If it is about communication, I agree on a clearer cadence and escalation path.

The result is not always that everyone gets what they wanted, but they understand the decision and their responsibility. That is usually enough to rebuild momentum."

Sandvik connection:
"The Customer Portal role will require this kind of coordination because many groups contribute to one service outcome."

## 9. Tell Us About A Time You Worked With A Difficult Stakeholder

Use when they ask:
"How do you manage a difficult stakeholder?"

Suggested answer:

"I have worked with stakeholders who were very demanding because they had customer pressure or business commitments behind them. I try not to label the person as difficult. I try to understand what pressure they are carrying and what they need to feel confident.

In one case, I improved the relationship by creating a clearer reporting rhythm: current status, risks, blockers, decisions needed, and next milestones. I also made sure I did not wait for the stakeholder to ask for updates when there was a risk. I communicated proactively.

The relationship improved because the stakeholder had more visibility and fewer surprises. My approach is to build trust through consistency, not just through agreement."

Sandvik connection:
"For a global customer portal, stakeholders may come from sales, marketing, regions, service, IT, and suppliers. Proactive transparency will be important."

## 10. Tell Us About A Time You Had To Handle Pressure

Use when they ask:
"How do you behave under pressure?"

Suggested answer:

"Under pressure, I try to create structure quickly. In service or delivery pressure, people can start working hard in different directions, so I focus on impact, priority, ownership, and communication.

In customer-facing platform work, when timelines or issues became urgent, I would first clarify the business impact and customer impact. Then I would identify the immediate actions, owners, and communication cadence. I also made sure risks and decisions were visible to leadership instead of keeping pressure only inside the team.

This helped teams stay focused and reduced confusion. I have learned that calm structure is one of the most useful things a delivery or service leader can provide under pressure."

Sandvik connection:
"For My Sandvik, pressure could come from incidents, supplier issues, releases, or customer-impacting portal problems. A Service Owner needs to stay calm and structured."

## 11. Tell Us About A Time You Improved Communication During An Issue

Use when they ask:
"Give an example of how you communicated during a difficult situation."

Suggested answer:

"In one delivery situation, different stakeholders had different versions of status because information was spread across meetings and tools. This created unnecessary tension because people were not working from the same picture.

I created a simple shared dashboard and status rhythm covering progress, blockers, risks, decisions, and next actions. I kept the language clear enough for both business and technical stakeholders. For urgent items, I made sure the update included impact, owner, ETA, and escalation need.

The result was better trust and faster decision-making. The main lesson was that communication is not only sending updates. It is making the situation understandable enough for people to act."

Sandvik connection:
"This is directly relevant to service reporting for portal SLAs, incidents, roadmap, suppliers, and improvement work."

## 12. Tell Us About A Time Security Or Compliance Created A Delivery Challenge

Use when they ask:
"How do you handle security or compliance constraints?"

Suggested answer:

"In payment and enterprise delivery environments, security and compliance were part of the delivery reality. Sometimes these requirements created tension because business stakeholders wanted speed, while security or audit expectations required more evidence, controls, or documentation.

My approach was to involve the right security or governance stakeholders early and make the requirement visible in the plan. I also tried to translate the requirement into concrete delivery actions: what documentation is needed, what control must be completed, who approves it, and by when.

This reduced last-minute surprises and made compliance part of normal delivery rather than something separate at the end. I am not a security specialist, but I know how important it is for the Service Owner to keep security visible in roadmap, release, supplier, and operational governance."

Sandvik connection:
"For My Sandvik, this matters because customer data, access, suppliers, integrations, and application standards all need disciplined ownership."

## 13. Tell Us About A Time You Had To Motivate People To Change A Process

Use when they ask:
"Tell us about resistance to change."

Suggested answer:

"When I introduced Kanban-based workflows and WIP limits to improve service request flow, not everyone immediately saw the value. Some people were used to working in their own way and worried that the new visibility would feel like extra control.

I explained the purpose as improving flow, reducing waiting time, and making blockers easier to solve. I also showed early data and examples where visibility helped the team get support faster. I listened to feedback and adjusted the process so it helped the team rather than becoming administration.

The result was a 50 percent improvement in turnaround time and better ownership of blocked work. My learning was that people accept process change more easily when they see how it reduces pain, not only when management asks for it."

Sandvik connection:
"This would be useful if Sandvik wants to improve portal service governance, support flow, or supplier follow-up."

## 14. Tell Us About A Time You Had To Balance Short-Term Fixes And Long-Term Improvement

Use when they ask:
"How do you balance immediate issue handling with continuous improvement?"

Suggested answer:

"In service delivery, there is always pressure to solve the immediate issue and move on. I try to separate three things: immediate workaround, permanent fix, and prevention.

For example, when customer-facing delivery or service requests were delayed, the immediate action was to unblock priority items and clarify escalation. The longer-term action was to improve the workflow, WIP limits, dashboards, and review cadence. That made the improvement sustainable instead of solving each request one by one.

The result was improved turnaround and better visibility. My approach as a Service Owner would be similar: restore the service quickly, but also use incident and request trends to improve the service over time."

Sandvik connection:
"For My Sandvik, that could apply to incidents, login issues, data quality, order flow, warranty support, or supplier delivery problems."

## 15. Tell Us About A Time You Had To Escalate

Use when they ask:
"When do you escalate, and how?"

Suggested answer:

"I escalate when a decision, risk, or dependency cannot be resolved at the working-team level and delaying escalation would increase business or customer impact. I try to escalate with facts, not emotion.

My escalation format is usually: what is the issue, what is the impact, what options have been tried, what decision is needed, what recommendation I have, and by when. I also make clear who owns the next action after the escalation.

This keeps escalation constructive. The goal is not to blame someone. The goal is to get the right decision at the right level before the issue affects delivery or service reliability."

Sandvik connection:
"In a Service Owner role, escalation discipline is important for suppliers, incidents, security issues, roadmap conflicts, and SLA risks."

## Strong Closing Line For Behavioural Answers

Use this when an answer needs a final sentence:

"The main learning for me was that difficult situations become easier to manage when ownership, facts, communication, and follow-up are clear."
